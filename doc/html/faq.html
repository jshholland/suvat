The Suvat v0.2 FAQ

Why is there a minus sign in the velocity (or displacement, or acceleration)?

You might initially think that velocity and speed are the same thing, but there is an important difference between them. That is that they velocity is a vector quantity while speed is a scalar. Similarly, distance is a scalar and displacement is a vector.

If you are not a mathematician or a physicist, that probably will not mean a lot. The key difference is that velocity includes direction as well as magnitude. One could say that a car has a speed of 50 mph, but to give its velocity one would have to say 50 mph North.

That makes sense on the two-dimensional ground, but the universe Suvat operates in is one dimensional (for the moment). This means that all motion that can be calculated in Suvat takes place along one infinitely long straight line. Velocity still has to include direction though, and direction is indicated by positive and negative numbers. Just imagine the line stretching out in front of you and say that going to the right is positive and to the left is negative. It doesn't really matter which way round they are, as long as you don't get confused between them.

As far as distance and displacement are concerned, pick a point on your infinitely long straight line. This is your origin, or zero point. All distances are measured from this point. If they are to the right, they might be positive and to the left, negative (depending on which way round you decide + and - are).

Why do I have to use SI units in Suvat?

Because SI units (metres, seconds and so on) are the best.

No really, that's why. When they were deciding on how long a second should be, and the size of other standard units, they realised that all the units in physics could be thought of in terms of 6 basic ones:

  • The kilogram (kg), the unit of mass
  • The metre (m), the unit of length
  • The second (s), the unit of time
  • The ampere (A), the unit of electric current
  • The kelvin (K), the unit of temperature
  • The candela (cd), the unit of light intensity
All the other units can be thought of as combinations of these basic ones (eg 1 N = (1 kg) * (1 metre) / (1 second)^2). The SI units therefore make it possible to use the simple formulae such as F = ma that relate the physical quantities and have the answers come out in the right units.

To be honest though, as the only units used in Suvat at the moment are metres and seconds, you can enter details in miles and hours if you like and have the velocities come out in mph. It's just a bit of a pain to work out how many miles 750 yards is.
